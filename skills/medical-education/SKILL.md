# Medical Education

## Overview

Design and evaluate health-professions education using an explicit educational problem, theory of change, reproducible intervention, valid outcome measures, appropriate design and analysis, and implementation evidence. Separate learner satisfaction, knowledge, skills, behavior, patient/system outcomes, and long-term transfer.

A polished teaching model or positive questionnaire does not by itself demonstrate educational effectiveness.

## When to use

Use this skill to:

- design curriculum, course, module, simulation, clinical teaching, assessment, faculty-development, or AI-enhanced education research
- build research questions, logic models, protocols, surveys, tests, rubrics, and evaluation plans
- select study designs and statistical methods
- audit teaching-reform manuscripts and grants
- analyze learner attitudes, knowledge, competence, behavior, implementation, and transfer
- plan qualitative or mixed-methods educational studies
- evaluate generative AI, knowledge graphs, virtual simulation, adaptive learning, or digital tools
- prepare reporting, ethics, data governance, and reproducibility records

## When not to use

Do not use this skill to:

- claim educational effectiveness from satisfaction alone
- treat natural classes as individually randomized groups
- fabricate student data, reliability, validity, effect sizes, attendance, grades, consent, or ethics approval
- use Cronbach's alpha as proof of construct validity
- infer causality from a one-group posttest or cross-sectional survey
- grade or discipline students through unvalidated opaque AI
- collect identifiable learner data without appropriate governance
- replace educational, statistical, psychometric, or ethical expertise when required

## Required inputs

Obtain or mark unresolved:

1. educational problem and context
2. learners, program, institution, course, year, and setting
3. curriculum/intervention and comparator/current practice
4. educational theory or conceptual framework
5. intended outcomes and time points
6. research design and unit of allocation/analysis
7. sample, classes/clusters, recruitment, and attrition
8. assessment instruments and psychometric evidence
9. implementation, fidelity, faculty, and resources
10. ethics, consent, privacy, grading, and power relationships
11. analysis software and reporting requirements
12. intended output: protocol, course design, paper, grant, survey, or evaluation report

Clarify whether the objective is educational improvement, research inference, quality assurance, program accreditation, or all of these with distinct governance.

## Outcome hierarchy

Classify outcomes explicitly:

- participation and reach
- reaction/satisfaction/acceptability
- attitudes, confidence, self-efficacy, and perceptions
- knowledge and retention
- skills and observed performance
- clinical reasoning and decision quality
- behavior in authentic practice
- patient, service, organizational, or system outcomes
- equity and access
- implementation and sustainability
- unintended consequences and harms

Do not present self-reported confidence as demonstrated competence.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the educational problem

Describe:

- current learner or system performance
- affected learners/stakeholders
- evidence and local data supporting the problem
- causes and contextual constraints
- consequence of no change
- educational versus structural components

Do not assume every performance problem is solved by more teaching. Workflow, staffing, incentives, assessment, supervision, or resources may be causal.

### 2. Define purpose and research question

Separate:

- curriculum-development objective
- implementation objective
- effectiveness/effect question
- measurement/validation question
- learner-experience question
- mechanism question
- transfer/sustainability question

Use a framework suited to the design. Avoid a single question that mixes attitudes, test scores, clinical behavior, and patient outcomes without a causal plan.

### 3. Select and operationalize theory

Choose a theory only when it informs design or interpretation. Examples may involve constructivism, cognitive load, deliberate practice, experiential learning, self-determination, social learning, communities of practice, reflective practice, technology acceptance, implementation frameworks, or program-evaluation models.

Specify:

- constructs
- intervention components linked to constructs
- mediators/mechanisms
- measurable indicators
- contextual moderators
- expected outcomes

Do not mention a theorist decoratively after the intervention is already designed.

### 4. Build a theory of change or logic model

```text
Context/inputs → Educational activities → Learner engagement/mechanisms → Immediate outcomes → Transfer/behavior → Patient/system outcomes
```

List assumptions and external influences. Patient outcomes require a credible causal chain and adequate design.

### 5. Review existing evidence

Use IMRS searches, reference verification, and evidence matrices. Assess similar learners, interventions, comparators, instruments, outcomes, implementation, and limitations.

Educational evidence may span biomedical and education databases. Do not assume PubMed alone is comprehensive for pedagogy.

### 6. Describe curriculum and intervention reproducibly

Record:

- objectives and competencies
- content and sequence
- educators/facilitators and training
- instructional methods
- learner tasks and workload
- duration, frequency, and dose
- technology and materials
- feedback and remediation
- assessment alignment
- adaptations and tailoring
- fidelity and exposure
- co-interventions and concurrent curriculum changes

Use an intervention-description framework when useful. A named model such as “blended learning” is not enough.

### 7. Select the comparator

Define usual teaching, waitlist, alternative curriculum, historical cohort, stepped rollout, or no comparator. Describe comparator content and exposure; “traditional teaching” is too vague.

Assess contamination, concurrent reforms, instructor effects, cohort differences, and unequal contact time.

### 8. Select the study design

Options include:

- individually randomized trial
- cluster randomized trial
- quasi-experimental controlled before–after
- interrupted time series
- stepped-wedge design
- nonrandomized cohort comparison
- one-group pretest–posttest
- cross-sectional survey
- longitudinal cohort
- qualitative study
- mixed-methods evaluation
- design-based research
- realist/implementation evaluation
- instrument-development/validation study

State the inference each design permits. A one-group improvement may reflect maturation, testing, regression, history, or concurrent teaching.

### 9. Define unit of allocation and analysis

Learners may be nested within classes, teams, instructors, sites, or programs. Record:

- allocation unit
- intervention-delivery unit
- outcome-measurement unit
- analysis unit
- number and size of clusters
- intraclass correlation assumptions

Do not analyze clustered allocation as independent individuals without adjustment.

### 10. Define sample and recruitment

Describe eligible learners, year/program, mandatory/elective status, recruitment, consent, incentives, participation, nonresponse, attrition, transfers, and exclusions.

Address power differentials when teachers recruit or assess their own students. Separate research consent from required course participation.

### 11. Justify sample size

Use the primary outcome, expected effect, variance, alpha, power, clustering, repeated measures, attrition, multiple groups, and analysis model. For qualitative studies justify sampling through information power or methodological logic.

Small cohorts may require estimation-focused, repeated-cohort, mixed-methods, or feasibility designs rather than exaggerated causal claims.

### 12. Develop or select instruments

For each instrument record:

- construct and intended use
- target population and language
- item source and permissions
- response scale and scoring
- content evidence
- response-process evidence
- internal structure
- relations with other variables
- consequences of testing
- reliability appropriate to use
- floor/ceiling effects and responsiveness

Do not treat face validity, expert review, Cronbach's alpha, KMO, or a significant Bartlett test as sufficient validation.

### 13. Develop questionnaires responsibly

Use literature, qualitative input, learner/stakeholder consultation, expert review, cognitive interviewing, pilot testing, item analysis, and prespecified scoring as appropriate.

Avoid double-barreled, leading, absolute, socially desirable, ambiguous, or jargon-heavy items. Plan missing-response handling and reverse-coded items cautiously.

When comparing programs or grades, verify measurement invariance before interpreting group differences in latent constructs.

### 14. Design knowledge and performance assessments

Create a blueprint linking objectives, content, cognitive level, format, and weight. Assess item difficulty, discrimination, distractors, reliability, standard setting, rater training, and scoring consistency as applicable.

For OSCE/simulation/workplace assessment, address stations/cases, raters, generalizability, checklist versus global ratings, authenticity, safety, and consequential validity.

Do not reuse exposed test items without considering practice effects and security.

### 15. Plan qualitative and mixed methods

Define paradigm/approach, sampling, interview/focus-group/observation process, reflexivity, coding, analytic method, negative cases, credibility, and data saturation/information power claims.

For mixed methods specify design, timing, priority, integration points, joint displays, and how qualitative evidence explains or challenges quantitative results. Do not run two parallel studies without integration and call them mixed methods.

### 16. Plan implementation and fidelity evaluation

Assess:

- reach and representativeness
- adoption by instructors/sites
- fidelity, dose delivered, dose received, and adaptations
- acceptability, appropriateness, and feasibility
- cost and workload
- contextual barriers/facilitators
- maintenance and sustainability
- unintended consequences

An intervention can fail because theory is wrong, implementation is weak, or measurement is insensitive; distinguish these possibilities.

### 17. Evaluate AI-enhanced education

For generative AI, agents, automated feedback, adaptive systems, virtual patients, or knowledge graphs, record:

- educational task and theory
- model/tool/version and access date
- prompts/workflow and instructor oversight
- training data or known limits when available
- accuracy and hallucination checks
- citation verification
- data privacy and retention
- bias, accessibility, language, and equity
- academic integrity and allowed use
- learner dependence and skill atrophy
- baseline digital/AI literacy
- comparator and equal instructional time
- reproducibility despite model updates

Do not treat frequency of use or satisfaction as proof of improved learning.

### 18. Protect ethics, privacy, and fairness

Address ethics review/waiver, consent, coercion, teacher–student power, grading separation, confidentiality, small-cell reidentification, recordings, AI/vendor data, accommodations, accessibility, and adverse educational consequences.

Use deidentified data and restricted access. State whether participation affects grades. Do not invent ethics approval.

### 19. Build the analysis plan

Align analyses with design and outcome:

- descriptive statistics and uncertainty
- baseline characteristics without significance rituals
- repeated-measures/multilevel models
- clustering and instructor/site effects
- covariate adjustment and prespecification
- missing data and attrition
- multiplicity
- effect sizes and confidence intervals
- psychometric analysis
- qualitative analysis
- mixed-method integration
- sensitivity analyses

Ordinal Likert items, summed scales, and latent constructs are not interchangeable. Justify treatment and assumptions.

### 20. Interpret pre–post and group comparisons

For controlled studies assess group-by-time interaction or appropriate model rather than declaring effectiveness because one group is significant and the other is not.

For one-group pre–post designs report improvement but acknowledge alternative explanations. Do not call it an RCT or causal proof.

### 21. Interpret correlations and prediction

Correlation among attitudes, self-efficacy, satisfaction, and intended use does not prove learning or causation. Address common-method bias, temporal order, construct overlap, multiple testing, and model validation.

Do not call a cross-sectional regression a predictive model of future behavior.

### 22. Report results completely

Include participant flow, exposure/fidelity, missing data, descriptive results, effect estimates with intervals, instrument performance, unintended effects, subgroup limitations, and qualitative/mixed-method integration.

Avoid reporting only p values or percentages without denominators. Do not hide null findings.

### 23. Interpret educational significance

Separate:

- statistical significance
- effect magnitude
- educational/practical significance
- retention
- transfer
- behavior
- patient/system impact
- feasibility and sustainability

Short-term knowledge gains do not prove durable clinical behavior or patient benefit.

### 24. Report using design-appropriate standards

Select and verify current reporting guidance appropriate to education, trials, observational studies, qualitative/mixed methods, simulations, instrument studies, and interventions. Explain context and deviations.

Reporting compliance does not prove methodological quality.

### 25. Produce deliverables

Return as requested:

1. education research brief
2. theory-of-change/logic model
3. aim–design–outcome matrix
4. intervention specification
5. assessment blueprint
6. questionnaire/instrument plan
7. implementation/fidelity plan
8. statistical and qualitative analysis plan
9. ethics/data-governance plan
10. reporting and reproducibility checklist

## Safeguards

- Never fabricate learners, data, effect sizes, reliability, validity, ethics approval, instruments, grades, attendance, or implementation.
- Never infer competence from confidence or satisfaction alone.
- Never call natural classes individually randomized.
- Never use alpha alone as validity evidence.
- Never infer causality from cross-sectional or uncontrolled designs.
- Never ignore clustering, repeated measures, instructor, or cohort effects.
- Never use significant-within-group versus nonsignificant-within-group as proof of between-group difference.
- Never expose identifiable learner data or use opaque AI for high-stakes grading without validation and governance.
- Never claim patient benefit from proximal learner outcomes without a supported causal chain.
- Verify unstable standards, technologies, institutional rules, and vendor data practices live.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Correction |
|---|---|
| Teaching activity is the research question | Define construct and inference |
| Theory named but unused | Map constructs to components and measures |
| “Traditional teaching” undefined | Specify comparator content and dose |
| Two classes called randomized | State cluster/nonrandom allocation accurately |
| Satisfaction called effectiveness | Add knowledge, skill, behavior, or system outcomes |
| Alpha/KMO/Bartlett called full validity | Build a validity argument for intended use |
| Pre/post improvement called causal | Address maturation, testing, history, and regression |
| One group significant, one not | Test the group difference/interaction |
| Likert item and scale treated identically | Define construct, scoring, and analysis |
| Mixed methods without integration | Specify integration and joint inference |
| AI usage frequency called learning | Measure educational outcomes and comparator |
| Patient benefit claimed from student scores | Bound outcomes and causal pathway |

## Reproducibility record

Record:

- educational problem, context, learners, course, and dates
- theory/framework and logic model version
- intervention and comparator materials
- educator training, fidelity, adaptations, and exposure
- design, allocation, clusters, sample, attrition, and flow
- instrument versions, permissions, scoring, and validity evidence
- assessment blueprint and item/rater quality
- ethics, consent, grading separation, privacy, and data access
- AI tool/model/version, prompts, oversight, and update effects
- analysis plan, software/code, assumptions, and deviations
- qualitative codebook and mixed-method integration
- results, implementation, unintended effects, and limitations

## Completion criteria

The education study is complete only when:

- educational problem, learners, context, and intended inference are explicit
- theory changes intervention, measurement, or interpretation
- curriculum and comparator are reproducible
- design and unit of analysis are accurate
- sample and clustering are addressed
- outcomes span the level required by the claim
- instrument validity evidence matches population and use
- statistical, qualitative, and mixed-method analyses match the data
- implementation, fidelity, context, and unintended effects are assessed
- ethics, power relationships, grading, privacy, and AI governance are credible
- conclusions distinguish satisfaction, learning, competence, behavior, and patient/system impact
- reporting and materials allow another educator to understand and reproduce the work
